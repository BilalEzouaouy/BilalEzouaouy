Pipe Flow Lab

Purpose:
This experiment involves the measurement of pressure losses in pipe flow to experimentally determine the friction factor. These values will be compared to values determined from accepted friction factor correlations. Three different types of pipe are employed in the experimental apparatus. Laminar, transitional, and turbulent pipe flow will be observed experimentally.

Background:
To balance the wall shear stress as a fluid flows through a pipe, pressure energy is lost. Although it is difficult to measure the wall shear stress, pressure differences are much easier to measure. Using pressure drop measurements, the friction factor, which is the non-dimensional wall shear stress, can be determined. The friction factor and pressure loss both increase with increasing surface roughness. Although the pressure drop increases with increasing flow rates, the friction factor decreases. Our objective in this lab is to quantify the friction factor in three different types of pipe (with three different surface roughness’s) and three different diameters of one type of pipe, for three different flow rates.

Experimental Procedure:
With the hose and pressure gage attached to one of the lengths of pipe, quantify the pressure drop and volume flow rate in the pipe. Volume flow rate will be determined by measuring the outflow volume over a measured time.

Figure 1: Pressure Drop vs Flow rate.

 

Large copper, Galvanized iron, and PEX pipes can achieve much higher flow rate with lower pressure drop compared to small and medium copper pipes. All pipes have an exponential or parabolic trend.

 

Figure 2: The friction Factor vs Reynold’s Number.

In terms of theoretical laminar friction factor, all types of tubing, including copper and PEX, displayed identical values. The copper and PEX tubing exhibited virtually indistinguishable theoretical friction factors. However, the galvanized iron tube demonstrated a distinct theoretical turbulent factor.

Laminar flow ends at Reynold’s Number of 2300 and turbulent begins around 4000.

The closer the measured values are to the transitional Reynold’s Number, the less similar they are to the theoretical values.
